Diary App UX Case Study

Driven by curiosity, I decided to inquire into the frustrations and joys of using a digital diary. This case study included qualitative research such as interviews and personas as well as a usability study on the Diario app.

Process: Interviews, Personas, Content Audit, Card Sorting, Experience Mapping
Software: Notion, Moqups, Adobe Xd, Adobe Illustrator, Canva, Miro

Approach

I began this study seeking to understand why people who want to keep a diary often find themselves unable to manage it. In order to answer this question, I began with secondary research from online sources to gain more background information on the problem. After becoming familiar with the problem space, I conducted 8 interviews on people who have tried to use a diary and would like to create the habit of diary use to develop a persona which would help me understand the user better. This information was then used for analysis of the Diario app.

Select Questions

  • When do people feel like they want to write in a diary?
  • What do people like about the experience of the diary?
  • What is the primary value of the diary in the minds of users?
  • What might stop people from using digital diaries?

Insights

From the interview provess I gained insights about user goals and pains which I then used to create the fictional persona of Alice Lu to help me empathize with potential diary app users.

Diario App Analysis

The Diario app analysis evalates the usability of the mobile app through a content audit, card sorting with a user who is representative of the persona, and a usabiilty study with the same user. With this information I will then draw conclusions about the ability of this app to serve user needs.

What is Diario?

Diario is a multi-platform diary app on android, ios and web. It offers users many different categorization and searching functions for their diary entries. This is done through creating folders, tags, keywords, and moods. Users are also able to attach geographic locations and pictures. The app is synced through dropbox between devices, however this feature requires a paid membership. The multilingual option is also available in Diario Pro, which costs eleven dollars annually.

Information Architecture and Content Audit

How usable and intuitive is the organization of the app?
Would Alice be able to navigate it while in a rush and potentially struggling with motivation to write?

For this study I will relate insights gained when comparing the content audit to the card sorting results. To view the full content audit and card sort, click here.

Card sorting done with sticky notes.

Overall, the app does an adequate job of organizing information such that the user can find it. However, there were points of confusion for the user. They felt that the sign in was likely to be its own entity, and not part of another menu. Additionally, they were confused by some of the naming such as “multiselect” and “atlas”. They also placed the template option in settings as opposed to in the top bar of the entry page. When naming the categories, the user was unsure of what to name the last one since they struggled to see the relevance of the items.


The user was confused about items in the top bar, but sorted the side bar content accurate to the app organization (screenshots from Diario app)

Experience Mapping

After a usability study, I create a user experience map to synthesize the knowledge gained and show where the app creates positive and negative experiences for the user. I have also included possible ideas for improving the app where there are pain points.


Evalutation and Next Steps

Overall, this app suceeds in the use of many features user were interested in such as searchability, prompts, and capacity for customization. However, the usability struggles could hinder users who are struggling with motivation. The app could benefit from reorganization of the information and re-evalutating the clarity of the naming.
